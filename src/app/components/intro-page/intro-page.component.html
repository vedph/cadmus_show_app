Introduction to Cadmus

What Is Cadmus?

In short, Cadmus is an open-source, tiny framework for building web-based, layered, modular, and user-friendly content creation systems for highly structured data in Digital Humanities. So, in this context Cadmus is first of all a way of thinking and modelling data; and then a proof-of-concept system prototype, which puts its philosophy at work with real-world projects.

It is somewhat easier to start with saying what Cadmus is not.

Cadmus is not going to tell you how to model your data; you are

Cadmus is not a prebuilt set of models you are forced to embrace to create your content. Rather, it is just a framework where you can place your own data models, together with their editing UI. So, these models are totally up to you. The architecture is designed to provide the skeleton to be embodied by the flesh of your own content. This provides a quicker path to its creation. Nonetheless, Cadmus being a modular system, you can take advantage of existing models and compose your own with minimal effort. This provides not only data, but even UI reuse, across the whole system.

Logical, not physical modeling

Cadmus is not an encoding format, too. In fact, its underlying storage technology is handled through an abstraction, so it can be replaced with anything else at any moment, without affecting the other layers in the system.

Thus, Cadmus is not going to replace your preferred models or technologies. It is just going to eventually support them, by providing a rapid development framework for creating highly structured content, without having to train users in any specific technology. Cadmus is more content-centric than technology-oriented; it focuses on domain models, rather than on the details of their digital encoding. Users do not need to mess with them.

Text as a datum, rather than a data architecture

Although mostly used with texts, Cadmus is not a text annotation system. It is content-agnostic, so you can well add text with any number of different and complex annotations (critical apparatus, orthography, abbreviations, paleographic features, comments, and whatever you might think of). Yet, text here is not a privileged structure bearing all the data connected to it, as it usually is the case with markup technologies like XML. Any type of content, either textual, meta-textual, or non-textual, can be equally created whithin the system, and whatever its nature it shares a common architecture. In fact, some of the projects adopting Cadmus have no textual content at all. By contrast, in a more traditional content creation process of Scholarly Digital Editions, a “text-centric” perspective is often responsible of the persistence of a “book paradigm” in its digital reincarnation. When producing content, we essentially deal with a text which flows from paper to a digital document with annotations (XML), to be then published to HTML via XSLT. Here, the tree structure laid on the text bears the whole data universe. This is consistent with the typical usages of TEI, essentially oriented to the “reconstruction of documents”. Yet, in some cases, especially with the outgrow of data provided by new types of analysis (e.g. morphological, syntactical or metrical tagging), and/or when handling very complex documents (e.g. inscriptions or complex literary traditions), this may not be the most efficient paradigm for creating content. In such scenarios a paradigm shift might be beneficial; and Cadmus aims to provide at least a concrete example of a tool which can effectively implement this shift in real-world projects.

Content vs Presentations

Cadmus is not usually targeted to direct publication. It belongs to the content creation stage of a project, and everything in it is optimized for this purpose. It aims to provide a user-friendly environment for collaborative, web-based content creation, whatever the models of your content.

So, for instance TEI might well be a product derived from Cadmus' content; but as the outcome of a more articulated production flow, rather than its starting point. Thinking textual, meta-textual or non-textual content beyond the technological and mental markup constraints allows freeing scholars from a number of practical issues (e.g. apparatus and workarounds like stand-off), letting them focus on their logical rather than physical models. Also, in creating content we should emphasize the typical requirements of any modern content editing infrastructure, like centralization, robustness, real-time validation and search, web-based concurrent editing, etc., which are very difficult to be met when dealing with text files.

In this context Cadmus is not even a point-to-point comprehensive solution; it rather stands at the center of a content production flow, which according to the complexity of the project may imply a number of preprocessing and import stages before it; and a number of indexing, transformations and exports after it. For some of these stages, e.g. importing legacy, often low-structured digital data, some solutions like my Proteus are designed to work side by side with it. At any rate, Cadmus is the editing framework which works on content, either from scratch, or derived from something else; and then usually produces something else in the context of a longer flow.

This approach implies that the database used in entering data is usually distinct from the one (if any) used to publish them. For instance, a typical use case is represented by a web application targeting end users, and drawing its data from a traditional RDBMS backend. In this scenario, the RDBMS database will be an automatic product of the data editing database; but the two databases are kept distinct, because they serve different purposes.

When entering data, we privilege an architecture widely open to expansion, with dynamic and modular modelling, and mostly fit to the requirements and habits of the scholars involved in the project. When publishing data instead, we will usually switch to a more rigid structure, typically implemented with a RDBMS, including any type of indexing and remodelling useful for the purposes of data publication and sharing.

In this context, the relational database of this sample is just one of the possible outputs derived from Cadmus content. Another one would be TEI, or a set of RDF triples, whatever the ontologies you are going to use; or PDF, static HTML, CSV, etc.

Content Flow

Bottom-up

Thus, Cadmus provides a way of working which in a sense is similar to the traditional, "bottom-up" approach, where scholars used to produce a set of documents representing the "dossier" about their research subject. Later, eventually they summarized their results by means of collecting and consolidating data from each of these documents, into a more structured and rigid scheme. The crucial difference is that in a pre-digital world they have been doing this by simply writing a set of documents in a word processor; here instead they they are producing highly structured content, by filling a number of forms, rather than just writing a free text.

Once this structured content is in place, you can do with it whatever you want; for instance, generate TEI documents, or RDF sets. In fact, often content is best created in a richer and more complex model, where only a subset of it gets extracted and remodelled into triples. This is much easier than striving to express any type of content, whatever its complexity and traditional scholarly description, directly into a set of triples.

Sample: extracting RDF from models

For instance, consider the trivial case of a linear physical measurement. In Cadmus, it might be represented with a simple model, which is both user-friendly and structured. This model includes 2 or 3 dimensions for width, height, and depth; each with a value and a measurement unit. Eventually, the user can tag each dimension (or the measurement as a whole) to categorize or group it in some way. This model is edited in the below UI; under it, you can find the model represented as an object with its properties, serialized in JSON format.

Try playing with the UI, and then click the button. You will get a fake CIDOC-CRM export, where a set of triples get exported from the data you entered. According to the CIDOC-CRM model, we focus on the measurement event, which includes several dimensions (for width etc.). Each of these dimensions has a value, a unit, and a type (width etc.).

Further, you can also specify your name. This of course is not part of the Cadmus model for the size; but is added here just to show you how the export can collect and consolidate data from different models. In this demo, when you enter a name you will get some additional triples, which tell that the user with a specific identifier is a person, and the measurement was taken by him/her.

So, here you can see a very small sample of this concept. The RDF export using a CIDOC-CRM ontology is a graph, where each datum gets atomized and linked to other nodes. This of course is the basis for the powerful semantic web model; but for human users, it would be rather painful to directly express themselves in this way. They usually want to create a huge quantity of structured content in a more organic scenario, where a number of "dossiers" about each topic gets created during their research. So, here Cadmus acts a bridge between the requirements of human researchers and those of data modeling and publication.

Cross-Integration

Integrating resources in the data flow

The above workflow could be envisaged as a "horizontal" flow, where content eventually coming from legacy or paper-based resources, or created from scratch, enter the Cadmus editing environment with its dynamic, modular and open modeling architecture, to be later exported in any desired output.

In some cases, we could also imagine another workflow crossing this one, where we also need to integrate some external resources into the editing experience. For instance, think of a bibliography: usually we can be satisfied with a bottom-up approach, where bibliographic entries are scattered across a number of "dossiers", to be later collected and consolidated into a unique resource. So this is a "bottom-up" approach, where bibliography is built from a set of different "documents".

Top-down

Anyway, there might be cases where we prefer a "top-down" approach, either because our references are so relevant that we want to build an independent bibliographic resource, or because we just want to leverage an existing bibliographic resource, like a dedicated RDBMS, or a public service like Zotero. In such scenarios, you can well integrate these resources into the editor, implementing a "top-down" approach, where you read (and eventually also write) data from an external resource.

This simple example about bibliography was chosen right because Cadmus already provides modules for both these approaches in this domain: a top-down bibliographic sub-system is already implemented and used by one of the Cadmus-based projects, side by side with other projects using a bottom-up approach. Anyway, that's only the iceberg's tip; the same approach could prove useful for any other publicly available resource, like e.g. geographic gazetteers, IIIF services, etc. As each data model in the system has its own editor, and this essentially is a miniature web application, there is no limit to their complexity and to the resources they might be using.

Thus, this provides an open editing system, where integration is a key feature either by means of import/export and by means of resources connection.
